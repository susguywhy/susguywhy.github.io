PTB (Summer of 2024)

 

I live (Nevada) about some 2000+ miles away from my parents back home. I recently purchased a vehicle which is one model year newer than the current vehicle that I am driving; a 2008 Ford Escape V6 (no more Hybrids, and you'll come to learn why)… The vehicle that this project is intended for is a 2007 Ford Escape Hybrid but I think it is applicable to all first generation Ford Escape Hybrids along with the first (one or two) year(s) of the second generation Ford Escape Hybrids; you need to have "the button.”

 

 

History…

The 2007 Ford Escape hybrid, from what I think, is a fairly good vehicle. Granted, when I purchased this vehicle, It had a bit less than 60,000 miles on the Odometer. It was locally owned by a Nevada business. I guess, I may be a bit biased when it comes to this vehicle so I'll just provide you with what I have experienced with my 2007 Ford Escape Hybrid and spare you my opinions of the vehicle as they are neutral at this point.

 

 

 

I've owned this vehicle for three years and during these years, I've had to replace the 12V DC car battery twice due to the Nevada heat and age of the original battery that came with the car. I've also upgraded the Low-beam headlights with some LED bulbs (which have not failed yet…) I don't remember the brand and I'm a bit lazy to look it up on Amazon – I think it costs me just $100 some time in 2022, when I first acquired the vehicle. I've changed the motor oil every 3000-4000 miles and I also changed the eCVT fluid around 70,000 miles. I used an ATF (Automatic Trans. Fluid) from Valvoline (red bottle) at the time. This is a 2WD variant so I did not do anything with Rear Axles or PTU as they don't have these components (as far as I know). Other than these items, nothing else was done to this vehicle – it has been fairly reliable for me so far…

 

The HVTB (High-Voltage Traction Battery) started to deteriorate throughout my ownership and gave me more grief when it came to reliability and it finally reached a breaking point for me. With 79,000 miles on the odometer, it got to the point where I'd keep a Battery Jump Pack (12V) in the trunk just to boost the 12V Car battery enough to charge the HVTB. If I let this car sit for two days without driving it (for at least 20 minutes), the HVTB would not have sufficient charge to start the engine and I would not be able to go anywhere. A fun fact about this car – it has a recovery button to instruct the computer to charge the HVTB from the 12V car battery with a push of a button (even without the key in the ignition). Apparently, this was a feature that was left in by Ford to allow a way for the owner to start their vehicle if it sat for a long period of time. I don't believe the car has a conventional starter but instead relied on the HVTB and electric motor to start the gasoline engine (a 2.3L Atkinson-cycle 4 cylinder engine)… some more basic information on how this vehicle worked can be found at this link. Anyway, way before this (I'd say for about a year and a half), the vehicle would experience, what I call, tremors and slow accelerator response. This typically happened when the car was started after sitting for at least several days and it would require you to step on the gas pedal fully in order to get the car to move at reasonable speeds. Turning at intersections took a little more skill as you had to floor the gas pedal ahead of time (by about 2 seconds) for the computer to realize that you really need help. Also, kinda dangerous…

 

This prompted me to try and get rid of this Hybrid. I thought about getting another Hybrid vehicle since the fuel efficiency was a nice-to-have but in the Nevada heat, the batteries are really cooked and abused during the summer months. I think this was what caused the problems I described above... as least, exacerbated it? I decided to purchase a used 2008 Ford Escape XLT v6 4WD after looking on several marketplaces online – this wasn't my first choice but it was a convenient one for me as I was already familiar with the Ford Escape and the powertrain for this variant was not much different than the prior year models. I purchased the ‘08 Ford Escape so that I could sell or give away the Hybrid. However, seeing as to how I travel to see my folks occasionally and renting a vehicle when I got there is expensive (especially during the holidays), I decided to ship the Hybrid to my folks to store in their garage and to use when I came to visit. This naturally lead to the question of how to deal with the HVTB while I'm away from my parents house. After consulting with a few colleagues and throwing different ideas around, the idea to use a computer board to periodically “press” the charge button in the Hybrid (so that the HVTB would not be left at zero for prolonged periods), was settled on. 

 

The Idea…

The Hybrid has a 12V AGM battery and is connected to a trickle charger (2A) to maintain the battery. Charging the HVTB involves pressing a button by the driver-side footwell and waiting up to 8 minutes for the computer to charge the HVTB from the 12V car battery. After the charge was completed, the HVTB would be at a sufficient level of charge to permit you to start the gasoline engine. Once the gasoline engine started, the HVTB battery would be charged by the car's alternator (I think…). The 12V AGB battery would then be trickled charged for the rest of the day (until the next time the Charge button was pressed).

 

 

 

How…

So the trickiest part of this project, I think, was to build a physical platform for the “button-presser” to rest on. Since the Charge button was also mounted vertically on the driver-side foot well, there wasn't a good place to mount a plunger-type device or even have leverage to slide an object into the button… this was probably overkill but I ultimately decided to use a servo with a tiny arm mounted to the cog. The servo would be mounted against a metal bracket with some metal-wire ties (I think it was literally copper wire) and the metal bracket would be screwed down onto a wooden platform. This wooden platform would fit the driver-side footwell and provide the stability needed when the servo activated. 

 

 

The Computer board chosen was the Arduino R4 WIFI; I needed a WIFI enabled board to monitor several parameters such as the voltage of the 12V battery and also track the amount of times the button is pressed remotely, while I was back in Nevada. To my surprise while perusing the web for a solution, I came across Arduino Cloud which offered the ability to monitor your projects remotely through the usage of exposed cloud variables (I think I settled for a second-highest package for 30/year). You can even download the Arduino IoT app which enables you to monitor your project on your phone! how cool is that? In addition to the Arduino and the Servo, a current and voltage sensor was also used; a module with the INA226 was used to measure the voltage from the car battery. Conveniently, the cigarette lighter in the Hybrid is wired directly to the car's 12V battery, so I purchased a cigarette plug to barrel jack adapter along with some (female) pigtails and wired everything on a small breadboard. I also wired in an LED (which I had left over from my days in university) to a GPIO pin to give some indication of operation. 

 

 

Update…

So, I switched out the Arduino R4 WIFI for an Arduino Nano 33 IoT board to free up the Arduino R4 for future projects. The Nano board appeared a little more primative (as it should) compared to the Arduino R4 but from a Cloud-usage perspective, I couldn't tell the difference (except maybe the load times over the air). The smaller computer board did present some minor challenges (as it wasn't a direct drop-in replacement for the R4) but these were overcome easily and quickly due to access to numerous libraries available for the Arduino ecosystem. The code that is published is a direct copy-and-paste from the online IDE that was used for development – so If you want this to work right “out of the box” (mostly), I suggest you copy and paste the source file into a blank ‘sketch’ in your Arduino Cloud account and fill in the blanks (Such as WIFI info and some cloud variables). The cloud variables are provided below and the dashboard I've created will allow you to visualize the 12V battery voltage along with several buttons to initiate actions on the Nano board such as Pushing-the-Button (charge HVTB) and resetting the board.

 

 

 

Some details of the code…

I've included an auto reset feature in the code for when the WIFI goes out; it should allow at least 5 minutes before the system is rebooted in the event the WIFI is lost. I discovered during development that the WIFI library will not attempt to reconnect once the connection goes out so I needed a way for the board to automatically detect this condition and initiate a reboot… You'll see from the layout that there's a wire going from one of the pins directly to the RESET pin of the Arduino Nano. 

 

 

In addition, the Arduino Nano would reboot numerous times a day when it was placed into the driver-side footwell... I didn't have too much time to debug this issue as I was in a time crunch to get back to Nevada, so I instrumented the usage of NTP and some flash memory to store the last time the charge button was pressed and the uptime of the system to determine if it was time to ‘press-the-button’, even if a spurious reset had occurred.

 

The loss of WIFI issue could have probably been easily remedied with some extra code to watch for a disconnect and to retry but I'm not familiar with WIFI and the Arduino WIFI Library so I opted for a crude reboot solution… As with the spurious reboots, I'm still working on that… will try to find the culprit when I am back at my parents' again.

 

 

Concerns…

Naturally, some concerns arose during the development of this project. The Servo and arm constitute a rotating lever which swings about the cog to “rub” up against the charge button. At one actuation a day, the long term effects of this lever arm acting on this charge button are unknown. Taking a guess, since the lever arm isn't pressing the charge button directly head-on, the rubbing of the arm against the button may eventually cause the button to fail. In the code, I've made the arm “throw ” a value that's based on the desired angle; this value can be tweaked once you get the metal bracket in place. Alongside this concern, I don't know the long term effects on the overall system when it comes to actuating this charge button on a daily basis. Maybe the Ford engineers only anticipated the usage of this button sparingly and in dire situations and not for maintenance of the HVTB in any way.

The HVTB may eventually fail and cause a fire. I purchased one automatic fire extinguisher and placed above the HVTB pack. Hindsight, I should have purchased at least 3 or more.

 

 

Next steps…

I'm going to see how this solution works for the next months as I am due back to my parents' soon and I will be monitoring the project remotely in the meantime. 

Things to improve: actually change out some of the cells within the HVTB pack (dangerous but do-able); Add more features to the code to allow for some intelligence when it comes to charging the HVTB such as HVTB voltage monitoring; also, but not limited to, directly charging the HVTB and bypassing the onboard 12V to HVTB charger. This latter fix has the added benefit of keeping the HVTB topped off the entire time; Improve the timing of the button press as it may drift – the timer which is responsible for counting down until the next button press is dependent on the Nano's internal clock/oscillator (not a crystal). It would be nice to key everything off the NTP timer.

bonus improvement 1: add a thermal imaging array (camera) to monitor the HVTB and check for hotspots. This would be something that needs to be implemented if direct HVTB pack charging is implemented. 

bonus improvement 2: add a feature to Read and Write to the HVTB module to initiate HVTB pack rebalancing via the CAN bus… I know right! it seems like this car has a lot of debug features itching to be exposed.
